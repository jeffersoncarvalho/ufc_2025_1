Hadean Eon

The Hadean Eon is the oldest eon in Earth's history, dating from about 4.6 billion to 4.0 billion years ago. During this period, the Earth was characterized by a partially molten surface, intense volcanism, and frequent asteroid impacts. The planet was incredibly hot due to gravitational compression, radioactive decay, and asteroid impacts, and much of this heat still exists inside the Earth today

The Hadean Eon marks the time of Earth's initial formation, including the accretion of dust and gases, collisions with larger bodies, and the stabilization of its core and crust. The atmosphere and oceans also began to form during this period.

Evidence suggests that liquid water may have been present on Earth as early as 4.4 billion years ago, and there might even be indications of life existing over 4.0 billion years ago, although the most reliable record for early life, the microfossil record, starts at 3.5 billion years ago.

The Hadean Eon ended approximately 4.031 billion years ago and was succeeded by the Archean Eon. The formation of the Moon, which is thought to have occurred due to a collision between Earth and a Mars-sized body, is believed to have happened during the early stages of the Hadean Eon.

Rocks from the Hadean Eon are very rare, with most of the available evidence coming from zircon grains found in the Jack Hills of Western Australia.

Archean Eon

The Archean Eon is the second of the four geologic eons of Earth's history, lasting from 4.031 to 2.5 billion years ago. It is named after the Greek word for beginning, arkhē. This eon is significant for the appearance of the earliest known life forms, primarily represented by shallow-water microbial mats called stromatolites, which remained simple prokaryotes throughout the eon.

The Earth during the Archean was mostly covered by water, with continental crust existing but mostly submerged under deep oceans. The atmosphere was a reducing atmosphere rich in methane and lacking free oxygen, with oxygen levels as low as 0.00001% of modern levels. However, transient episodes of heightened oxygenation occurred around 2,980–2,960 Ma, 2,700 Ma, and 2,501 Ma.

The Archean Eon is subdivided into four eras: the Eoarchean (4031 to 3600 Mya), Paleoarchean (3600 to 3200 Mya), Mesoarchean (3200 to 2800 Mya), and Neoarchean (2800 to 2500 Mya

The beginning of the Archean Eon is marked by the Late Heavy Bombardment, an event where a large spike of asteroids and comets impacted the Earth and Moon, creating many of the Moon's craters.

Eoarchean Era

The Eoarchean (IPA: /ˌiːoʊ.ɑːrˈkiːən/ EE-oh-ar-KEE-ən; also spelled Eoarchaean) is the first era of the Archean Eon of the geologic record. It spans 431 million years, from the end of the Hadean Eon 4031 Mya to the start of the Paleoarchean Era 3600 Mya. Some estimates place the beginnings of life on Earth in this era, while others place it earlier. Evidence of archaea and cyanobacteria date to 3500 Mya, comparatively shortly after the Eoarchean. At that time, the atmosphere was without oxygen and the pressure values ranged from 10 to 100 bar (around 10 to 100 times the atmospheric pressure today)

Paleoarchean Era

The Paleoarchean (/ˌpeɪlioʊ.ɑːrˈkiːən, ˌpæl-/ PAY-lee-oh-ar-KEE-ən, PAL-), also spelled Palaeoarchaean (formerly known as the early Archean), is a geologic era within the Archean Eon. The name derives from Greek "Palaios" ancient. It spans the period of time 3,600 to 3,200 million years ago. The era is defined chronometrically and is not referenced to a specific level of a rock section on Earth. The earliest confirmed evidence of life comes from this era, and Vaalbara, one of Earth's earliest supercontinents, may have formed during this era.

  • Acastian Period, 4031–3810 Mya
  • Isuan Period, 3810–3600 Mya

Mesoarchean Era

The Mesoarchean (/ˌmiːzoʊ.ɑːrˈkiːən, ˌmɛzoʊ-/ MEE-zoh-ar-KEE-ən, MEZ-oh-, also spelled Mesoarchaean) is a geologic era in the Archean Eon, spanning 3,200 to 2,800 million years ago, which contains the first evidence of modern-style plate subduction and expansion of microbial life. The era is defined chronometrically and is not referenced to a specific level in a rock section on Earth.

  • Vaalbaran Period, 3490–3020 Mya
  • Pongolan Period, 3020–2780 Mya

Neoarchean Era

The Neoarchean (/ˌniːoʊ.ɑːrˈkiːən/ NEE-oh-ar-KEE-ən; also spelled Neoarchaean) is the last geologic era in the Archean Eon that spans from 2800 to 2500 million years ago—the period being defined chronometrically and not referencing a specific level in a rock section on Earth. The era is marked by major developments in complex life and continental formation.

  • Methanian Period, 2780–2630 Mya
  • Siderian Period, 2630–2420 Mya

Proterozoic Eon

The Proterozoic Eon is a significant period in Earth's history, spanning from 2.5 billion to 541 million years ago, marking the second division of the Precambrian time and preceding the Phanerozoic Eon. This eon is characterized by profound changes in the planet's atmosphere, lithosphere, and biosphere, setting the stage for the complex life forms that would emerge in subsequent eras. During the Proterozoic, modern plate tectonics became active, and the ancient cores of the continents moved over wide areas of the globe, accumulating smaller fragments of crust and sometimes colliding with other large landmasses. The Proterozoic is often divided into three distinct eras: the Paleoproterozoic (2.5 to 1.6 billion years ago), Mesoproterozoic (1.6 to 1 billion years ago), and Neoproterozoic (1 billion to 541 million years ago).

Each era is marked by unique geological and biological developments, including the gradual accumulation of oxygen in the atmosphere and the emergence of multicellular organisms

Paleo Proterozoic Era

The Paleoproterozoic Era (also spelled Palaeoproterozoic) is the first of the three sub-divisions (eras) of the Proterozoic eon, and also the longest era of the Earth's geological history, spanning from 2,500 to 1,600 million years ago (2.5–1.6 Ga). It is further subdivided into four geologic periods, namely the Siderian, Rhyacian, Orosirian and Statherian.

  • Siderian Period, 2500 Mya - 2300 Mya
  • Rhyacian Period, 2300 Mya - 2050 Mya
  • Orosirian Period, 2050 Mya - 1800 Mya
  • Statherian Period, 1800 Mya - 1600 Mya

Meso Proterozoic Era

The Mesoproterozoic Era[4] is a geologic era that occurred from 1,600 to 1,000 million years ago. The Mesoproterozoic was the first era of Earth's history for which a fairly definitive geological record survives. Continents existed during the preceding era (the Paleoproterozoic), but little is known about them. The continental masses of the Mesoproterozoic were more or less the same ones that exist today, although their arrangement on the Earth's surface was different.

  • Calymmian Period, 1600 Mya - 1400 Mya
  • Ectasian Period, 1400 Mya - 1200 Mya
  • Stenian Period, 1200 Mya - 1000 Mya

Neo Proterozoic Era

The Neoproterozoic Era is the last of the three geologic eras of the Proterozoic eon, spanning from 1 billion to 538.8 million years ago,[2] and is the last era of the Precambrian "supereon". It is preceded by the Mesoproterozoic era and succeeded by the Paleozoic era of the Phanerozoic eon, and is further subdivided into three periods, the Tonian, Cryogenian and Ediacaran.

  • Tonian Period, 1000 Mya - 720 Mya
  • Cryogenian Period, 720 Mya - 635 Mya
  • Ediacaran Period, 635 Mya - 538.8 Mya

Phanerozoic Eon

The Phanerozoic Eon is the current and most recent geological eon, spanning approximately 541 million years from the present to the end of the Proterozoic Eon. It is characterized by the proliferation and diversification of abundant animal and plant life on Earth. The term "Phanerozoic" is derived from the Ancient Greek words φανερός (phanerós), meaning "visible," and ζωή (zōḗ), meaning "life," reflecting the eon's rich fossil record

The Phanerozoic is divided into three major eras: the Paleozoic, Mesozoic, and Cenozoic, each marked by distinct geological and biological developments. The Paleozoic Era, or "ancient life," began 541 million years ago and ended 252 million years ago. The Mesozoic Era, or "middle life," lasted from 252 million to 66 million years ago. The Cenozoic Era, or "recent life," began 66 million years ago and continues to the present.

The Phanerozoic Eon is significant because it marks the transition from largely sessile Precambrian biota to the active mobile life forms of the Cambrian period and beyond. The Cambrian Explosion, which occurred at the beginning of the Phanerozoic, saw a massive biodiversification of multicellular organisms. Since the systematic study of the Ediacaran period began in the 1950s, trace fossils of complex soft-bodied metazoa from this period have been identified, indicating that complex life existed at least since the Tonian period of the Proterozoic eon.

The Phanerozoic Eon represents only about 12% of Earth's total history, with the remaining 88% being the Precambrian eons (Hadean, Archean, and Proterozoic).

Paleozoic Era

The Paleozoic (/ˌpæli.əˈzoʊ.ɪk, -i.oʊ-, ˌpeɪ-/ PAL-ee-ə-ZOH-ik, -⁠ee-oh-, PAY-;[1] or Palaeozoic) Era is the first of three geological eras of the Phanerozoic Eon. Beginning 538.8 million years ago (Ma), it succeeds the Neoproterozoic (the last era of the Proterozoic Eon) and ends 251.9 Ma at the start of the Mesozoic Era.[2] The Paleozoic is subdivided into six geologic periods (from oldest to youngest), Cambrian, Ordovician, Silurian, Devonian, Carboniferous and Permian. Some geological timescales divide the Paleozoic informally into early and late sub-eras: the Early Paleozoic consisting of the Cambrian, Ordovician and Silurian; the Late Paleozoic consisting of the Devonian, Carboniferous and Permian.

Mesozoic Era

The Mesozoic Era[3] is the era of Earth's geological history, lasting from about 252 to 66 million years ago, comprising the Triassic, Jurassic and Cretaceous Periods. It is characterized by the dominance of archosaurian reptiles such as the dinosaurs, and of gymnosperms such as cycads, ginkgoaceae and araucarian conifers; a hot greenhouse climate; and the tectonic break-up of Pangaea. The Mesozoic is the middle of the three eras since complex life evolved: the Paleozoic, the Mesozoic, and the Cenozoic.

Cenozoic Era

The Cenozoic Era (/ˌsiːnəˈzoʊ.ɪk, ˌsɛn-/ SEE-nə-ZOH-ik, SEN-ə-;[1][2] lit. 'new life') is Earth's current geological era, representing the last 66 million years of Earth's history. It is characterized by the dominance of mammals, insects, birds and angiosperms (flowering plants). It is the latest of three geological eras of the Phanerozoic Eon, preceded by the Mesozoic and Paleozoic. The Cenozoic started with the Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event, when many species, including the non-avian dinosaurs, became extinct in an event attributed by most experts to the impact of a large asteroid or other celestial body, the Chicxulub impactor.
